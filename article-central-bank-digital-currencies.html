Central Bank Digital Currencies: Where Do Major Economies Stand?

By Daniel Nakamura February 28, 2026

The race to develop central bank digital currencies has intensified dramatically, with over 130 countries now actively exploring digital versions of their sovereign currencies. What began as academic curiosity following Bitcoin's emergence has evolved into a geopolitical priority as nations recognize CBDCs' potential to reshape monetary policy transmission, financial inclusion, and international payment systems. The stakes extend far beyond technological modernization—CBDCs may fundamentally alter the architecture of global finance.

China has established clear leadership in CBDC development among major economies. The digital yuan has expanded from limited pilot programs to deployment across twenty-six cities, processing transaction volumes exceeding 100 billion yuan. Beijing views the e-CNY as instrumental to reducing dependence on U.S. dollar-dominated payment networks while maintaining comprehensive oversight of domestic financial flows. The digital yuan's programmability features—enabling targeted stimulus payments and conditional spending—represent capabilities no physical currency can match.

The European Central Bank's digital euro project has progressed to its preparation phase, with launch anticipated by 2028. European policymakers emphasize privacy protections and payment system resilience as primary motivations, seeking to reduce dependence on non-European card networks while preserving cash as a parallel payment option. Design decisions around anonymity thresholds and commercial bank disintermediation risks continue to generate debate among member states with varying financial system structures.

The Federal Reserve approaches CBDC development with characteristic caution. Despite advancing Project Hamilton's technical research in partnership with MIT, the Fed has not committed to issuing a digital dollar. Fed officials emphasize the need for Congressional authorization and express concern about potential banking system disruption. The U.S. enjoys incumbent advantages in global payments through dollar dominance and established correspondent banking networks, reducing urgency compared to countries seeking alternatives to existing infrastructure.

Emerging economies have often moved faster than developed nations. Nigeria launched the eNaira in 2021, becoming one of the first countries to deploy a retail CBDC. The Bahamas' Sand Dollar and the Eastern Caribbean DCash have operated for several years, providing real-world implementation lessons. Brazil's digital real pilot programs have attracted significant attention from financial institutions seeking exposure to programmable money capabilities.

Cross-border CBDC applications represent perhaps the most transformative potential. Current international payments remain slow, expensive, and opaque—a legacy of correspondent banking arrangements designed decades ago. Multiple initiatives, including mBridge connecting Asian central banks and Project Dunbar coordinating between Singapore, Australia, Malaysia, and South Africa, are testing CBDC interoperability for wholesale settlements. Success could dramatically reduce transaction costs and settlement times for international commerce.

Privacy and surveillance concerns persist as significant obstacles to CBDC adoption. Citizens in democracies express wariness about government visibility into transaction activity, even when technical privacy protections exist. Design decisions around transaction monitoring, anonymity thresholds, and data retention will significantly influence public acceptance. Central banks must balance financial crime prevention obligations against legitimate privacy expectations—a tension with no easy resolution that will shape CBDC evolution for years to come.
